EUROPEAN MASTERPIECES

A Journey Through Architectural Excellence

Paris, France

NOTRE-DAME DE PARIS

1163 - 1345

Built on the Île de la Cité, Notre-Dame has been a centerpiece of Paris for over 850 years. The cathedral is known for its Gothic architecture, with flying buttresses and twin bell towers that define the Parisian skyline.

The rose windows are particularly famous - massive circular stained glass designs that fill the interior with colored light. After the 2019 fire, restoration work continues to preserve this historic monument for future generations.

Notre-Dame represents one of the finest examples of French Gothic architecture, with intricate stone carvings and architectural innovations that influenced cathedral design across Europe.

Barcelona, Spain

SAGRADA FAMÍLIA

1882 - Present

Gaudí's unfinished basilica combines Gothic and Art Nouveau forms. The building has been under construction for over 140 years, with completion expected in the coming decades.

The design features tall spires dedicated to religious figures, with intricate facades depicting biblical scenes. Inside, columns branch out like trees, creating a forest-like atmosphere with natural light filtering through stained glass.

Still being built using Gaudí's original plans, the Sagrada Família attracts millions of visitors annually and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Budapest, Hungary

HUNGARIAN PARLIAMENT

1885 - 1904

Located along the Danube River, the Hungarian Parliament is one of Europe's largest government buildings. It blends Neo-Gothic style with Renaissance and Baroque elements.

The central dome rises 96 meters high - a reference to the year 896 when Hungarian tribes arrived in the region. With 691 rooms and 29 staircases, the building houses the National Assembly and the Hungarian Crown Jewels.

242 sculptures decorate the exterior, showcasing Hungarian rulers and military leaders. Built during the Austro-Hungarian Empire, it symbolizes Hungarian national identity and independence.
